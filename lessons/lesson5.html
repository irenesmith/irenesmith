Lesson 4 covered left-borders, now in Lesson 5 we're to create a double border. There are (at least) two ways to create a page that has a double border, and this is the first.

I created three background graphics for this page. The left border, the right border, and a tiny tile for the background to make sure it matches the small piece of background on the inner edge of the borders. The whole thing has been set up in a table with three columns, one for each part of the border. I used a clear graphic for content in those columns to prevent browsers (such as Netscape) from collapsing the empty cells.

The center column contains a table that is 90% of the width of the cell it is in, with a center alignment so that the text moves away from the borders. As long as the browser windows doesn't get so narrow that the borders are forced to overlap the content, this page will look good at any resolution.

The design in the border is yet another piece out of one of my clip art collections. The thin gold border and the shadows were aded by me using Corel PaintShop Pro X and the Flaming Pear filter Super BladePro. I created the title, the buttons, and the bar by taking pieces from the diamond part of the border which I cut out and resized appropriately as I needed it.

The second way to do this is to use a single background tile and center the content in the middle of it. This gives an entire different look to the page. You can see what I mean on this page that uses what the instructors call the "cheat" method.
